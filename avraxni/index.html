Grammar

Syntax

If you don't already know what syntax is, it is the order that words go in, in order to make sense. There are 4 important things I need to cover: basic word order, adjective order, adverb order, & genitive (pro)noun order.

Pronouns

If you would like to know what the case abbreviations mean, please refer to Noun Declension. I will now explain the pronoun abbreviations.

Personal Pronouns

1st person pronouns are pronouns like: I, me, we, & us. I / me is the singular 1st person pronoun, and so would be written as 1SG. We / us is the plural, so it'd be notated with 1PL. 2nd person pronouns include: you & y'all. 2SG = you, 2PL = y'all. Avraxni doesn't actually have a 2PL pronoun, but I just felt like I should mention it. You getting the hang of this? Good, now time to introduce you to animacy.

Animacy refers to whether something is "alive" or not. Animate pronouns then, are like: he, she, & (singular) they. I abbreviate animate as AN. Since these are also 3rd person pronouns, we'd write she, he, & they as 3SGAN. You may have noticed that he, she, & they are in the same pronoun. This is because there are no gender distinctions in Avraxni, including in pronouns. Inanimate pronouns are things that aren't "alive". This is where the it pronoun comes in. I know many people identify with the pronouns it/its, so I'd like to say that I don't believe that those pronouns are always inanimate. I simply use "it" as an example of inanimate pronouns. The 3rd person plural pronoun does not make any animacy distinction, so it'd just be written as 3PL.

Now to explain clusivity. With the 1st person plural pronoun, "we/us", there's a bit of ambiguity about who it refers to sometimes, specifically about whether or not you are inCLUded. If I say "we just bought a house", 1: you'd think I'm from a different planet with cheaper housing prices and want me to take you there, and 2: "we" could refer to "you & I" or "me & someone else". We don't use separate words for these concepts in English, and it's usually pretty easy to figure out what it means by context. However, things are diffferent in Avraxni. This distinction is made with "inclusive we" referring to "you & I" or "all of us", and "exclusive we" reffering to "us but not you". I write these as 1PLINCL for inclusive we & 1PLEXCL for exclusive we.

Noun Declension

Noun declension is the changing of form for nouns to show some sort of grammatical feature. If you've ever learned about verb conjugation, it's like that but with nouns. In Avraxni, you have to change the ending of a noun to convey case & the ending must agree in animacy.

So what is case? Well, there are different functions that nouns have in a sentence. The roles that nouns play are always shown with prepositions and syntax in English. There are 5 declined cases for nouns in Avraxni. Nominative (NOM), genitive (GEN), associative (ASC), lative (LAT), & locative (LOC). That was a lot of big words all at once, so let's break each of these down.

What is the nominative case? It is the subject of a sentence. It's hard to describe what a subject is so I'll just show you. In bold are the subjects of these sentences: "I threw an apple", "The car drove itself away", "Her name was written on the plaque". The subject is usually what goes before English verbs. The nominative case is just assumed so u don't change the noun for this case.

The genitive case is much easier to explain. It shows the possessor of another noun. It's something like "the ant's antenna" & "my pickle jar" but a genitive (pro)noun cannot be used on its own. So, in "The bag of chips aren't your's", there is no genitive case being used. Technically, when it's used this way in English, it's said to be in the possessive case.

The cases that are left are very easy to explain so here we go! Associative case basically just means "with {noun}" but it's also used to show possessive statements. Saying "the bread is his" would translate as "the bread is with him".

The lative case, in Avraxni, is used in 2 ways. It basically means "to {noun}" and also shows the recipient of a verb. An example of this is in the sentence "The IRS sent you a strongly worded letter". In this sentence "you" is the recipient and "letter" is the object. Also in this sentence, "you" is probably the recipient of some fines as well.

The locative case means "at {noun}" and also shows the direct object of a sentence. I am putting all the direct objects in these sentences into bold letters. I hope you can figure it out if I keep doing this for a while.

Now you should have a good idea of what cases are, and how they're used in Avraxni. The itty bitty chart below shows all the endings you must attach to the noun for each case and don't forget that they must agree with animacy.

Verb Conjugation

Verb conjugation is the change in form of a verb to mark some grammatical function. In Avraxni, verbs conjugate for direct object, tense, & aspect. I'm just gonna assume that you know what direct objects & tenses are.

Aspect

There are three main aspects in Avraxni: Imperfective, perfective, & habitual. What is aspect? Well, verb tense shows at what point in time a verb happens: in the past, present, & future. However, sometimes you need more information. Verb aspect in a nutshell shows how a verb takes up time. To see what I mean, I'll show you some examples. Think about the difference between "I walk on the beach" & "I am walking on the beach". The difference is aspect. The first one is more ambiguous on how it takes up time, but the second one is pretty clear. "Walking" conveys that the event is ongoing or progressing continuously through time. Let's get into the Avraxni aspects.

Imperfective means that a verb is not an instant complete action and it emphasizes the process of an action rather than the completion. On the other hand, there's the perfective. The perfective refers to a complete action & emphasizes its completion. If you look at the charts, you may notice that perfective aspect has no present tense. This is because the present is ongoing. When you finish the present, it just becomes the past. Habitual aspect is the last one. It's pretty easy to understand compared to the others, don't worry. If a verb is in the habitual aspect, it means that the verb is done by the subject on a regular basis. Think about "I study Spanish". In this sentence, study would be in the habitual aspect because studying is something you do regularly. I abbreviate each of the aspects as IMP, PRF, & HAB.

If ur getting confused on how I abbreviate the pronouns, please check out the pronouns section of the page.
